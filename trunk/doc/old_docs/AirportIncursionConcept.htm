Airport Incursion Concept Document

 

Overview

 

This document seeks to provide a general understanding of how each user interface will be laid out to maximize utility while still maintaining an excellent level of user-friendliness.  In the airport project, there were six user roles that had to be considered as the graphical user interface (GUI) was designed.  As the project revolved around an air traffic control tower and the different aspects involved within, the primary roles incorporated into the project include: Flight Data/Clearance Delivery, Ground Control, Local Control, Helicopter Control, Traffic Manager, and a Supervisor/Administrative position.

 

 

Supervisor/Administrative

 

Necessary Components:

 

á      Access to all screens

á      Override ability

á      Specific attention focus on intersections between Tower Cab controls

á      Monitor intersections between different systems

á      Scheduling

á      Access to registration (Administrative role)

á      Outside effects monitoring (i.e. environmental, unforeseeable obstacles)

 

Summary: The Supervisor/Administrator (S/A) has the most demanding and important role in the control tower and inherently will require the most complex user interface.  The S/A must be able to simultaneous monitor all key areas (areas most likely to have issues) while managing outside factors as well.  If a situation does occur in a key area, the S/A will require a necessary override ability to interrupt any operations occurring in the area.  In addition to this, the S/A will act as an administrator to the entire system, allocating specific tasks to other individuals through the registration. 

 

Traffic Manager

 

Necessary Components:

 

á      Listing of all airplanes in queued for arrival and departure

á      Listing of all available runway resources

á      Ability to route all existing aircraft

á      Capability to increase/decrease arrival and departure speeds

 

Summary: The Traffic Manager will be responsible for the general flow of air traffic at the airport.  They must be able to see all planes queued for arrival and departure, and the ability to change patterns and orders where necessary to maximize runway efficiency.  The Traffic Manager position is essentially one of optimization, where they will seek to move aircraft around the airport as responsibly possible.

 

Helicopter Control

 

Necessary Components:

 

á      Listing of all active helicopters within flight paths and airport vicinity

á      Ability to reroute active helicopters to new locations

á      Listing of all inactive helicopters in the vicinity

 

Summary:  The Helicopter control position manages all helicopters in the airport vicinity in order to ensure that they maintain a safe distance from aircraft.

 

Local Control

 

Necessary Components:

 

á      Ability to communicate freely with Traffic Manager

á      Ability to stall/(ÒGive go aroundÓ) current aircraft traffic in case of emergencies

 

Summary:  Local Control manages all surfaces within the airport.  Local essentially ensures that all aircraft are ensured safe passage throughout the airport, unhindered. 

 

Ground Control

 

Necessary Components:

           

á           Ability to stop and start taxiing services on the runway

á           Route and schedule taxiing services

á           Knowledge of all available taxis

á           Knowledge of incoming/outgoing flights within a given time period

 

Summary:  Ground Control literally acts like a modern taxi service.  It has knowledge of all potential destinations within a given area, and attempts to operate as efficiently as possible to meet the demands of aircraft flow.

 

Flight Data/Clearance Delivery

 

Necessary Components:

           

á      Should have a knowledge of the flight queue within a given time period

á      Ability to delay pilots(i.e. place into holding pattern)

á      Should have communication abilities with all pilots in airport vicinity

á      Queues pilots to depart/land

á      Ability to view runway status

 

Summary: FD/CD maintains the basic communication with all pilots in the airport vicinity.  They guide pilots in all aspects relevant to their flight status before they take off or land at the airport.
